# Negotiation Tactics Reference

Detailed breakdown of each tactic: the psychology, how to run it, example phrasing, and failure modes. Read the relevant section(s) for the situation at hand - you don't need all of them for every negotiation.

---

## Anchoring

**What it is:** The first number said in a negotiation becomes the reference point everyone measures against, even when they know it's arbitrary.

**Why it works:** The brain treats the first number as a mental starting line. Every subsequent number gets judged relative to it rather than on its own merits - this holds even for people who are aware of the effect.

**How to use it:**
- Go first whenever you reasonably can.
- Anchor bold but believable - extreme enough to shift the range in your favor, not so extreme it reads as a joke and costs you credibility.
- Selling / asking for more (salary, rate, price): anchor high.
- Buying / paying: anchor low.

**Script (salary example):** "Based on the scope of this role and market rates for it, I'm looking at $X." (Pick X near the top of the credible range for the role, not your minimum.)

**Failure mode:** Anchor too high/low and you look uninformed or not serious, and you may not get a serious counter at all.

## Mirroring

**What it is:** Repeating the last few words the other person said, back to them, as a question.

**Why it works:** Hearing your own words echoed back triggers an automatic urge to explain further. People elaborate to fill in what they assume you didn't understand - and in elaborating, they reveal information and often soften their position, without you having made any counter-argument.

**How to use it:** Take the last 2-4 words of what they said, repeat with a slight upward/questioning inflection. Say nothing else. Let them fill the silence.

**Script:**
- Them: "I can't go lower than $500."
- You: "Lower than $500?"
- (They elaborate, often revealing flexibility or the reasoning behind the number.)

**Failure mode:** Overusing it in the same conversation makes it obvious and stops working - use it sparingly, at moments where you actually want more information.

---

## Tactical Silence

**What it is:** Saying nothing after the other person finishes talking or makes an offer.

**Why it works:** Silence is socially uncomfortable. People are wired to fill dead air, and when you don't, the other side's brain fills the gap by second-guessing themselves - often sweetening the offer, revealing a concern, or talking past the point they should have stopped at.

**How to use it:** After they state a number or offer, pause. Five to ten seconds, longer if you can hold it. Stay calm and neutral - don't fidget or signal discomfort. Let them speak next.

**Failure mode:** None really, beyond your own discomfort - this is nearly risk-free. The main skill is just tolerating the pause instead of rushing to fill it yourself.

## The Flinch

**What it is:** A visible reaction of surprise or mild disappointment the moment you hear their number - before you say a word of counter-argument.

**Why it works:** People testing a price or demand are often unsure if they set it right. A genuine, visible reaction of surprise plants doubt - they start wondering if they overshot, and often walk the number back on their own.

**How to use it:** React authentically the instant you hear the number - a slight pause, raised eyebrows, a short exhale, or a verbal "Oh - okay, that's higher than I expected." Then let it sit (pairs well with tactical silence).

**Failure mode:** A faked or exaggerated flinch reads as theater and costs you credibility. Only use it where the number genuinely surprises you, or where you can make the reaction land as authentic.

## Good Cop / Bad Cop

**What it is:** One party plays rigid and unreasonable; a second (or the same person, invoking someone else) plays warm and on-your-side. The "relief" of dealing with the good cop makes you want to give them concessions.

**How to recognize it being used on you:** Watch for phrases like "I'd love to do this, but my manager would never approve it" - the invisible bad cop is often not a real obstacle, just a device to make the visible person feel like an ally while extracting concessions from you.

**How to use it yourself:** If there's a real second party or constraint (a budget, a partner, a boss) you can honestly invoke, use it: "I'm on board with this, but I'd need to run it by [X] - what's the best number I can bring them?" This is only ethical when the constraint is real; don't invent a fictional decision-maker.

**Failure mode:** If the other side clocks that the "bad cop" is fictional, trust collapses for the rest of the negotiation.

## Door in the Face

**What it is:** Open with a large, likely-to-be-rejected ask. After they say no, follow with the smaller ask you actually wanted - it now looks modest by contrast and triggers reciprocity (they feel they should also move toward you).

**How to use it:** First ask should be a genuine stretch - big enough to plausibly get rejected, not so absurd it reads as a joke. The real ask follows immediately after the rejection, framed as the reasonable alternative.

**Script:** "Would you be able to do [big ask]?" → "No, that's not possible." → "Understood - what about [actual target] instead?"

**Failure mode:** If the first ask is obviously a joke, the contrast effect doesn't land and you may lose credibility for the real ask.

## BATNA (Best Alternative to a Negotiated Agreement)

**What it is:** Your backup plan if this negotiation falls apart entirely - another job offer, another buyer, other quotes lined up.

**Why it's foundational:** A strong BATNA lets you negotiate from real power - you can hold firm and walk away without fear, and that confidence is visible to the other side. A weak BATNA shows up as visible desperation (fast concessions, eagerness, accepting bad terms) regardless of what tactics you use on top of it.

**How to use it:**
- Before negotiating, build a real BATNA where possible: apply to multiple roles, get multiple quotes, line up more than one option.
- Never bluff a BATNA you don't have - if tested and it collapses, you lose all leverage and credibility at once.
- If your BATNA is currently weak, say so plainly to the user and steer them toward lower-risk tactics (mirroring, silence, trading) rather than tactics that require a credible walk-away.

## The Nibble

**What it is:** After the deal is essentially agreed, asking for one more small thing - free shipping, an extra day, a small fee waived.

**Why it works:** By the time the terms are locked in, the other side has already made a mental commitment and doesn't want to reopen or blow up the deal over something minor. Saying yes to a small ask feels cheaper than restarting.

**How to use it:** Wait until the main terms are genuinely settled. Introduce the ask as an afterthought, not a renegotiation: "Oh, one more thing - could we include [small item]?"

**Failure mode:** Ask for something too large at this stage and you risk reopening the whole deal, which defeats the purpose.

---

## The Ultimatum

**What it is:** A hard line - take it or leave it, final offer - that forces an immediate decision and ends back-and-forth.

**When it works:** Only when you have genuine leverage and a real BATNA you're actually willing to use. It signals you've reached your limit and further discussion won't move you - and the other side believes it because you're prepared to walk.

**When it backfires:**
- Used too early, before trust or terms have been explored - reads as aggressive.
- Used as a bluff - if called, you either cave (losing all credibility) or lose a deal you wanted.
- Even when accepted, it can generate resentment that damages the ongoing relationship - worth weighing for negotiations that aren't one-off (e.g. with a manager you'll keep working with).

**Rule of thumb:** Don't advise a user to deliver an ultimatum unless they're genuinely prepared to walk away and live with that outcome.

---

## Trading, Not Giving

**What it is:** Never hand over a concession for free - every move toward their position is conditional on a move toward yours.

**Why it works:** Free concessions read as weakness and invite more asks. Concessions that cost the other side something in return feel earned to them, and are valued more highly - while also keeping the negotiation balanced so both sides are visibly invested in the outcome.

**How to use it:** Respond to any ask with a conditional: "I could do [their ask] if you're able to [something in return]." This isn't a refusal - it reframes their request as a trade rather than a gift.

**Script:** "I can lower it to that number if you can commit today / increase the order size / cover the difference in another line item."

**Applies throughout:** This isn't a one-time move like the others - it's a standing rule to apply any time a concession is requested, from opening through the nibble stage.
